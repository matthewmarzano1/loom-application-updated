Why did the number of work tasks change?

More tasks at the same cost still drives spend up. We need to strip out the part of the volume increase you can't control - fleet size - so what's left is the volume your team can actually move.

Out of your control Fleet size change
Why this is uncontrollable: If the business adds units, more work tasks naturally follow. Your team doesn't decide how many units the company operates, so the tasks generated by fleet growth alone aren't a fair target for cost reduction.

How we model it: We hold tasks-per-unit flat at the prior-year rate, then ask: "If we'd had this many units last year, how many tasks would we have expected?" The difference between that and actual current-year tasks is what's left to attack.

Why did the cost per work task change?

Labor contracts and parts pricing usually aren't yours to negotiate. We quantify how much of the cost-per-task increase comes from those two, and whatever's left over is on your team.

Out of your control Labor rate changes
What this captures: The YoY change in the hourly or fully-loaded labor rate from your labor agreement - collective bargaining outcomes, market adjustments, benefits inflation. Your team executes the work, but doesn't set the rate.

How we model it: Multiply the prior-year cost per task by labor's share of that cost, then by the rate change %. That gives the labor inflation impact per task, which we scale up by current-year task volume.
Out of your control Parts price changes
What this captures: The YoY change in unit prices for the parts you buy - vendor price increases, raw-material inflation, supply-chain pass-through. This is price, not usage. Usage (how many parts you consume per task) stays in the controllable bucket.

How we model it: Same pattern as labor - prior-year cost per task × parts share × price change %, scaled by current-year tasks.
Your team's bucket Everything else in rate
What this captures: Whatever movement in cost-per-task remains after labor inflation and parts inflation are stripped out. In practice this includes parts usage per task (using more or premium parts where standard would do), labor hours per task (efficiency, job plan adherence, rework), vendor and sourcing mix (which suppliers and outside shops you use), scope per work order, first-time-fix rate (rework drives effective cost up), and overhead allocation.

This is the bucket where your team's decisions show up. We'll compute it automatically from the residual.
